The Fire

Everyone knows California's wildfires keep getting bigger. The data says the opposite, and that is what hides the real danger.

File 02 — the tail

A few monsters
burn the state.

Here is why the average lies. Destruction is wildly lopsided. Plot all 3,118 fires by size and nearly every one piles up tiny on the left. A handful, the complex fires like the August Complex at over a million acres alone, get flung far to the right. Those rare monsters, not the typical fire, are what burn California.

File 03 — the drift

Toward people.
Into winter.

Now the part that should change how you read the next headline. Step through the years, or press Play and watch the map scar over. Through 2020 and 2021 the giants burned in the empty northern interior. By 2025 the burden swung south. Southern California's share of acres jumped to roughly 40 percent, including the January Los Angeles fires that erased whole neighborhoods.

And the season broke open

Fire is also escaping its season. For most of the decade the embers cluster on the summer and fall arc. But the outer rings glow almost the whole way around, including the dead of winter. The bright embers near the top of the 2025 ring are the January Palisades and Eaton fires.

Fewer wildland acres, but closer to where we live and outside the months we plan for.

04
The takeaway

Stop counting acres.

The typical California fire is shrinking, yet the risk is rising. A few unpredictable megafires drive nearly all the destruction, and that destruction is moving into cities and into winter. Acres burned tells you the past was bad. The distribution tells you where to prepare next: for rare catastrophic events, at the wildland-urban edge, all year long.

The question. How has the destruction caused by California wildfires changed from 2015 to 2025? The easy answer is that fires keep getting bigger. Our data pushed back. The typical fire has gotten smaller and far more frequent, which hides three things that matter more: a handful of rare megafires drive almost half of all acres burned, the damage is shifting toward the populated south, and fire is spilling outside its normal season into winter.

The views. Five linked D3 visualizations, each carrying one beat of the argument: a bar-and-line chart, a swarm of all 3,118 fires, a concentration bar, a linked map with an animated burn scar and a regional stack, and a calendar spiral. We used a square-root scale so a million-acre fire does not swallow the map, and held to a single fire color so intensity stays legible.

The challenge. Making a counterintuitive argument legible. The honest signal is a distribution, not one rising line, so we built views that show both the shrinking typical fire and the dominating tail at once. Tuning scales, keeping 3,000-plus records responsive, and matching county names across datasets all took real iteration.
