Finding 01

Fires got smaller, and far more common.

Start with two numbers, side by side. The bars are the total acres burned each year; the ember line is how many separate fires there were. They pull apart. The count climbs to record highs in 2024 and 2025, while total acreage stays well below the 2020 peak. Faster detection and suppression now catch most fires while they are still small.

Finding 02

A handful of monsters do nearly all the damage.

Here is why "average fire size" lies. Destruction is wildly lopsided. Plot every one of the 3,118 fires by size and almost all of them pile up tiny on the left. A few — the complex fires like the August Complex, over a million acres on its own — are flung far to the right. Those rare monsters, not the typical fire, are what burn California.

So far

The average fire is harmless. Risk lives entirely in the rare tail — the megafire you cannot predict by counting.

Finding 03

The threat is moving toward people, and into winter.

Now the part that should change how you read the next wildfire headline. Step through the years, or press Play and watch the map scar over. Through 2020 and 2021 the giant fires burned in the sparsely populated northern interior. By 2025 the burden swung south — Southern California's share of acres jumped to roughly 40%, including the January Los Angeles fires that erased whole neighborhoods.

Fire is also escaping its season. The spiral unspools the calendar into a coil: January at the top, the year running clockwise, each loop moving outward from 2015 at the center to 2025 on the rim. For most of the decade the embers cluster on the summer-and-fall arc, exactly when you would expect. But the outer rings glow almost the whole way around — including the dead of winter. The bright embers near the top of the 2025 ring are the January Palisades and Eaton fires.

So far

Fewer wildland acres, but closer to where people live and outside the months we plan for.

The takeaway

Stop counting acres.

The typical California fire is shrinking, yet risk is rising — because a few unpredictable megafires drive nearly all the destruction, and that destruction is moving into cities and into winter. Acres burned tells you the past was bad. The distribution tells you where to prepare next: for rare catastrophic events, at the wildland-urban edge, all year long.

About this project

The question. How has the destruction caused by California wildfires changed from 2015 to 2025? The easy answer is that fires keep getting bigger; our data pushed back. Working from CAL FIRE's archive of 3,118 fires, we found the typical fire has gotten smaller and far more frequent — which sounds like good news, but hides three things that matter more: a handful of rare megafires drive almost half of all acres burned, the damage is shifting toward the populated south, and fire is spilling outside its normal season into winter.

The visualizations. Built in D3 across linked views, each carrying one beat of the argument. A combined bar-and-line chart shows acres falling while counts rise. A beeswarm of all 3,118 fires makes the long tail visible, and a concentration bar shows ten fires owning forty percent of all acreage. A map plots every fire as an ember sized by acres, with a Play button that animates the decade and leaves a cumulative burn scar; a stacked bar breaks acreage down by region, linked to the map. A calendar spiral reveals the season stretching open. We used a square-root scale so a million-acre fire does not swallow the map, and held to a single ember accent so intensity stays legible.

The challenge. Making a counterintuitive argument legible. The honest signal here is a distribution, not one rising line, so we designed views that show both the shrinking typical fire and the dominating tail without contradicting each other. Tuning scales, keeping 3,000-plus records responsive under tooltips and animation, and matching county names across datasets all took real iteration.
