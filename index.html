The Shrinking Fire

Why "acres burned" hides the real story of California wildfire risk, 2015–2025.

Prabhjas Singh · Sohan Shingade · Nirav Kanthed  |  Data: CAL FIRE incident archive (3,118 fires)

Every year the headlines say the same thing: California wildfires keep getting bigger. But when you look at all 3,118 recorded fires from 2015 to 2025, the data says something more surprising. The typical fire is actually getting smaller — and that is exactly what makes the real danger easy to miss.

Finding 1

Fires got smaller, and far more common

Compare two things side by side. The bars are the total acres burned each year. The red line is the number of separate fires. They pull apart: the count climbs to record highs in 2024 and 2025, while total acreage stays well below the 2020 peak. Better detection and faster suppression now catch most fires while they are still small.

Finding 2

A handful of monsters do almost all the damage

Here is why "average fire size" lies. Destruction is wildly lopsided. Out of 3,118 fires, just 10 account for 42% of every acre burned in eleven years. The top 50 account for nearly three quarters. The 2020 and 2021 acreage spikes were not a thousand fires getting worse — they were a few enormous complex fires, like the August Complex, which alone burned over a million acres.

Finding 3

The threat is moving toward people, and into winter

Now the part that should change how you think about risk. Use the controls to step through the years, or press Play. Watch where the fires cluster. Through 2020 and 2021 the giant fires burned in the sparsely populated northern interior. By 2025 the burden swung south: Southern California's share of acres jumped to roughly 40%, including the January Los Angeles fires that destroyed entire neighborhoods.

The takeaway

"Acres burned" is the wrong headline. The typical California fire is shrinking, yet risk is rising, because a few unpredictable megafires drive nearly all the destruction, and that destruction is moving into cities and into winter. Counting acres tells you the past was bad. Reading the distribution tells you where to prepare next: for rare catastrophic events at the wildland-urban edge, all year long.

About this project

The question and the story

We set out to answer how the destruction caused by California wildfires has changed from 2015 to 2025. The easy answer is that fires keep getting bigger, but our data pushed back on that. Working from CAL FIRE's incident archive of 3,118 fires, we found that the typical fire has gotten smaller and far more frequent. That sounds like good news, but it hides three things that matter more: a handful of rare megafires drive almost half of all acres burned, the damage is shifting toward the populated south, and fire is spilling outside its normal season into winter. We built the page to walk a reader through that argument one step at a time.

How the visualizations support the argument

The project uses D3 and five linked views, each carrying one beat of the story. A combined bar and line chart shows total acres falling while fire counts rise. A concentration bar makes the lopsidedness obvious by showing that ten fires own forty percent of all acreage. A California map plots every fire as a circle sized by acres, and a stacked bar breaks acreage down by region. The map, the year buttons, and the region bars are all linked, so filtering one updates the others, and a Play button animates the decade so you can watch the burden swing south. A month-by-year heatmap reveals the season stretching open. We chose a square-root scale for the circles so that a million-acre fire does not visually swallow the map, and we kept the color palette restrained so region and intensity stay readable.

What was most challenging

The hardest part was making a counterintuitive argument legible. The honest signal in this data is a distribution, not a single rising line, so we had to design views that show both the shrinking typical fire and the dominating tail without contradicting each other. Tuning the scales, keeping over 3,000 records responsive with tooltips and animation, and matching county names across our fire data and the map boundaries all took real iteration. The result is a page we think changes how a reader interprets the next wildfire headline.
